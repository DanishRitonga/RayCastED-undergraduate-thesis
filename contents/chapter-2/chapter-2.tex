\section{Literature Review}

This section reviews prior work on nuclei detection and segmentation in histopathology images, focusing on methods most relevant to the proposed RayCastED model. The reviewed works are organized chronologically, tracing the evolution from polygon-based segmentation to end-to-end detection frameworks.

\subsection*{StarDist}

StarDist \cite{schmidt2018cell} introduced the star-convex polygon representation for cell detection, originally developed for fluorescence microscopy. In their extended work \cite{weigert2022stardist}, Weigert and Schmidt adapted StarDist to histopathology images, demonstrating its applicability to the CoNIC challenge where it achieved first place in the segmentation and classification task. StarDist predicts a set of radial distances from each cell centroid to its boundary at fixed angular intervals, producing a star-convex polygon that preserves boundary geometry. However, StarDist relies on a two-stage pipeline: it first generates a dense probability map and then applies non-maximum suppression (NMS) to filter overlapping predictions. This NMS step risks undercounting in densely clumped regions where overlapping predictions are suppressed, and the dense prediction approach introduces computational overhead from post-processing.

\subsection*{CPP-Net}

CPP-Net \cite{chen2023cppnet} addressed a key limitation of earlier polygon-based methods by improving how contextual information is used during polygon prediction. Rather than predicting the centroid-to-boundary distances from a single centroid pixel, CPP-Net samples a point set within each cell and fuses their predictions using a Confidence-based Weighting Module. The authors also introduced a Shape-Aware Perceptual (SAP) loss that constrains predicted polygon shapes. CPP-Net achieved state-of-the-art results on DSB2018, BBBC06, and PanNuke datasets. Despite its improvements in prediction robustness, CPP-Net still operates within a dense prediction framework with NMS-based post-processing, inheriting the same limitations as StarDist regarding clumped cell separation and computational overhead.

\subsection*{LKCell}

LKCell \cite{cui2024lkcell} proposed an efficient approach to nuclei instance segmentation by leveraging large convolution kernels to achieve large receptive fields with reduced computational cost. The method transfers pre-trained large convolution kernel models to the medical domain for the first time and designs a new segmentation decoder based on these kernels. LKCell achieved state-of-the-art performance (0.5080 mPQ) on PanNuke with only 21.6\% of the FLOPs compared to the previous leading method, demonstrating that FCN-based architectures can be both accurate and computationally efficient. However, LKCell follows a traditional dense prediction paradigm and does not address the NMS dependency or the clumped cell separation problem, as it focuses primarily on computational efficiency rather than the detection-accuracy trade-off.

\subsection*{CellPose-SAM}

CellPose-SAM \cite{stringer2025cellposesam} combined the CellPose framework with the Segment Anything Model (SAM) to achieve what the authors describe as superhuman generalization for cellular segmentation. By leveraging SAM's powerful visual features, CellPose-SAM demonstrates strong cross-domain performance without requiring extensive domain-specific training data. While its generalization capability is impressive, CellPose-SAM is a generalist segmentation model rather than a lightweight detection framework, and its computational profile makes it less suitable for real-time edge deployment in resource-constrained clinical settings.

\subsection*{LSP-DETR}

LSP-DETR \cite{pekar2026lsp} represents the most direct precursor to this work. It introduced a fully end-to-end framework for nuclei segmentation that represents nuclei as star-convex polygons within a DETR-style transformer architecture. By using a learned assignment mechanism instead of NMS, LSP-DETR eliminates the undercounting problem in clumped regions. The authors also proposed a novel radial distance loss that allows overlapping nuclei segmentation to emerge naturally. LSP-DETR demonstrated strong generalization across tissues and was over five times faster than the next-fastest leading method. However, its transformer-based backbone imposes quadratic complexity, making it computationally demanding for edge deployment. RayCastED builds directly on LSP-DETR's polygon representation and end-to-end assignment mechanism while replacing the transformer backbone with an FCN to achieve a lightweight computational profile suitable for resource-constrained environments.

\subsection*{Summary and Research Opportunity}

The reviewed methods reveal a clear trade-off landscape: polygon-based methods (StarDist, CPP-Net) preserve boundary geometry but depend on NMS; efficient FCN methods (LKCell) reduce computation but do not address NMS or boundary geometry; generalist models (CellPose-SAM) generalize well but are computationally heavy; and end-to-end transformer methods (LSP-DETR) eliminate NMS but rely on expensive transformer backbones. No existing method combines polygon-based cell representation, NMS-free end-to-end detection, and lightweight FCN-based computation. RayCastED addresses this gap by adapting LSP-DETR's polygon and assignment framework to an FCN backbone, targeting deployment on resource-constrained edge hardware.


\section{Theoretical Basis}

\subsection{Histopathological Foundations}

Histopathology is the study of tissue disease through microscopic examination of tissue samples. In clinical practice, tissue specimens are collected through biopsy or surgical resection and processed for microscopic analysis. The tissue undergoes fixation (typically in formalin) to preserve cellular structures, followed by embedding in paraffin wax, sectioning into thin slices, and mounting on glass slides for staining and visualization.

The cell nucleus is a membrane-bound organelle containing genetic material and serves as the primary structure of interest in histopathological analysis. Nuclear morphology, including size, shape, texture, and spatial distribution, provides critical diagnostic information. In cancer diagnosis, malignant cells often exhibit nuclear atypia: enlarged nuclei, irregular shapes, hyperchromasia (increased staining intensity), and abnormal mitotic figures.

A fundamental challenge in nuclei analysis is that nuclei in tissue sections are densely packed and frequently overlap, forming clumped clusters where individual boundaries are difficult to distinguish even for expert pathologists. This overlapping occurs because tissue sections are typically cut at a thickness of 3--5 $\mu$m, which is comparable to or smaller than the diameter of many nuclei (typically 5--10 $\mu$m). As a result, multiple nuclei at different depths within the section are projected onto the same 2D image plane, creating the appearance of clumped or touching nuclei. This is not merely an artifact of imaging resolution but an inherent property of projecting 3D tissue architecture onto a 2D plane, motivating the development of automated methods capable of both accurately delineating individual nuclear boundaries and correctly separating touching or overlapping nuclei.

\subsubsection{Tumor-Infiltrating Lymphocytes and Clinical Significance}

Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) are immune cells, primarily lymphocytes and plasma cells, that have migrated into the tumor microenvironment. The presence and density of TILs reflect the host immune response against the tumor and have been established as a prognostic biomarker in multiple cancer types. Higher TILs levels are generally associated with improved outcomes, particularly in triple-negative and HER2-positive breast cancers, where they predict better response to chemotherapy and longer survival.

The International TILs Working Group, convened by Salgado et al. \cite{salgado2015evaluation}, established standardized guidelines for TILs assessment in clinical practice. The recommended metric is \textbf{stromal TILs (sTILs)}, defined as the proportion of stromal area occupied by mononuclear inflammatory cells within the invasive tumor border:

\begin{equation}
	\text{sTILs (\%)} = \frac{\text{Area of mononuclear inflammatory cells in stroma}}{\text{Total intratumoral stromal area}} \times 100
\end{equation}

Only lymphocytes and plasma cells are included; polymorphonuclear leukocytes are excluded. Areas of necrosis, crush artifact, fibrosis, and hyalinization at the tumor border are also excluded from the assessment. The score is reported as a single average percentage per tumor on H\&E-stained full-tissue sections.

Clinically, tumors with sTILs $\geq$ 50\% are classified as lymphocyte-predominant breast cancer (LPBC), indicating a strong immune response. Additional commonly used thresholds include sTILs < 10\% (TILs-low) and sTILs $\geq$ 30\% (TILs-high in many triple-negative breast cancer studies).

This scoring methodology is fundamentally an \textit{area-based} measurement: it requires accurately delineating the boundary of each lymphocyte to compute its area contribution, not merely detecting its presence or counting its centroid. This distinction has direct implications for the design of automated nuclei analysis systems. Object detection models that output only bounding boxes or centroids are insufficient for sTILs scoring because they discard boundary geometry. Accurate sTILs computation requires methods that can both detect individual cells and delineate their boundaries, particularly in densely clumped regions where cell boundaries are ambiguous.

\subsection{Digital Pathology Imaging}

\subsubsection{Tissue Slicing and Scanning}

The digitization of histopathology slides produces whole-slide images (WSIs) through specialized scanners that capture high-resolution images of the entire tissue section. Modern slide scanners, such as those from Hamamatsu, Leica, and 3DHistech, use line-sensor or area-sensor cameras mounted on motorized stages to systematically capture overlapping tiles that are subsequently stitched into a single large image.

WSIs are typically stored in multi-resolution pyramidal formats (e.g., Aperio SVS, Hamamatsu NDP, or generic TIFF), where each level provides a different magnification. The spatial resolution of a WSI is specified in microns per pixel (MPP), which depends on the objective lens magnification used during scanning. Common resolutions range from approximately 0.25 MPP (equivalent to 40$\times$ magnification) to 0.5 MPP (20$\times$). At 0.25 MPP, a typical biopsy slide can produce an image exceeding 50,000 $\times$ 50,000 pixels, necessitating patch-based processing strategies for computational analysis.

\subsubsection{Staining and its Variants}

Tissue staining is essential because most biological tissues are nearly transparent under standard brightfield microscopy. Stains introduce contrast by selectively binding to tissue components through chemical interactions. The most widely used staining protocol in histopathology is Hematoxylin and Eosin (H\&E) staining.

Hematoxylin is a basic (positively charged) dye that binds to acidic structures such as DNA and RNA, coloring nuclei dark blue or purple. Eosin is an acidic (negatively charged) dye that binds to basic structures such as cytoplasmic proteins and extracellular matrix, coloring them pink. The combination produces the characteristic appearance of H\&E-stained tissue: blue-purple nuclei against a pink cytoplasmic and stromal background.

Beyond H\&E, several specialized stains are used in clinical practice: Periodic Acid-Schiff (PAS) for basement membranes and glycogen, Masson's Trichrome for collagen fibers, and immunohistochemical (IHC) stains for specific protein markers. Each stain produces distinct color characteristics that can be mathematically decomposed for computational analysis.

\subsubsection{Beer-Lambert Law and Optical Density}

The interaction of light with stained tissue follows the Beer-Lambert law, which describes the relationship between light absorption and the concentration of absorbing material. For a stained tissue sample, the transmitted intensity $I$ at a given wavelength is related to the incident intensity $I_0$ by:

\begin{equation}
	I = I_0 \cdot e^{-\alpha \cdot c \cdot d}
\end{equation}

where $\alpha$ is the molar absorptivity (extinction coefficient), $c$ is the concentration of the stain, and $d$ is the path length through the tissue. The optical density (OD) is defined as:

\begin{equation}
	\text{OD} = -\log_{10}\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) = \alpha \cdot c \cdot d \cdot \log_{10}(e)
\end{equation}

In the context of digital pathology, the RGB pixel values of a scanned image are proportional to transmitted light intensity. Converting from RGB to optical density space is essential because OD is linearly additive: if multiple stains are present, the total OD at each pixel equals the sum of the OD contributions from each individual stain. This linearity property underpins color deconvolution methods.

\subsubsection{Stain Vectors}

A stain vector represents the characteristic color signature of a specific stain in optical density space. For an RGB image, each stain is described by a 3-element unit vector $\mathbf{s} = [s_R, s_G, s_B]^T$ in OD space, where each component indicates the stain's relative absorption at the red, green, and blue channels.

For H\&E staining, the two stain vectors are:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Hematoxylin vector} $\mathbf{s}_H$: characterizes the absorption pattern of hematoxylin, which strongly absorbs in the blue-green range, producing its characteristic dark blue appearance.
	\item \textbf{Eosin vector} $\mathbf{s}_E$: characterizes the absorption pattern of eosin, which absorbs primarily in the green range, producing its pink appearance.
\end{itemize}

When two stains are present, the stain matrix $S$ is formed by concatenating the stain vectors as columns: $S = [\mathbf{s}_H \quad \mathbf{s}_E]$. For three-stain protocols, a third column is added.

\subsubsection{Stain Vector Estimation Methods}

Since stain appearance varies across scanners, laboratories, and tissue types, the stain vectors must be estimated from each individual image or dataset. Several methods exist for automatic stain vector estimation:

\textbf{Macenko's method} estimates stain vectors by analyzing the distribution of pixel OD values in the image. Pixels are converted to OD space, and those with extreme OD values (typically the top 1\%) are identified. Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is then applied to these pixels, and the two principal angles corresponding to the strongest eigenvectors define the stain directions. The resulting vectors are then projected onto a predefined stain color space to assign hematoxylin and eosin identities.

\textbf{Vahadane's method} uses non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) to simultaneously estimate the stain matrix and the stain density maps. This approach enforces sparsity constraints and non-negativity, which are physically meaningful since stain concentrations cannot be negative.

The choice of estimation method affects downstream stain normalization quality. Macenko's method is widely used due to its simplicity and robustness, while Vahadane's method produces more accurate separations at the cost of higher computational complexity.

\subsubsection{Color Deconvolution}

Color deconvolution is the process of separating an H\&E-stained image into its individual stain channels. Given the stain matrix $S$ and the OD image $\mathbf{v}$ (a pixel vector in OD space), the stain concentrations $\mathbf{c}$ for each pixel are obtained by solving the linear system:

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{v} = S \cdot \mathbf{c}
\end{equation}

Since $S$ is a $3 \times 2$ matrix (for H\&E) or $3 \times 3$ (for three stains), the system can be solved using the pseudoinverse:

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{c} = S^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{v}
\end{equation}

where $S^{-1}$ denotes the pseudoinverse (or inverse, for square $S$). The resulting stain concentration maps $\mathbf{c}$ provide separate grayscale images representing the hematoxylin and eosin density at each pixel. The hematoxylin channel is particularly useful for nuclei detection algorithms, as it isolates nuclear staining from cytoplasmic and stromal background.

\subsubsection{Stain Normalization}

Stain normalization addresses the significant color variation that exists across WSIs captured from different scanners, hospitals, or staining batches. This variation, if uncorrected, degrades the performance of deep learning models trained on data from one source and applied to another.

The standard approach to stain normalization involves three steps: (1) estimate the stain matrix $S_{\text{source}}$ from the source image using one of the methods described above, (2) compute the stain concentration map $\mathbf{c} = S_{\text{source}}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{source}}$, and (3) reconstruct the normalized image using a target stain matrix $S_{\text{target}}$: $\mathbf{v}_{\text{normalized}} = S_{\text{target}} \cdot \mathbf{c}$, then convert back from OD to RGB space.

This process preserves the structural content of the image while standardizing color appearance. Stain normalization is commonly applied as a preprocessing step before training or inference in computational pathology pipelines.
